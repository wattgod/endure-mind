# Post-Race Debrief — Guided Audio Script

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Lesson:** 5.6 — After The Line
**Voice:** Gentler than the other scripts. The athlete just finished a race — they're tired, emotional, possibly disappointed. Meet them where they are. Still the Stoic Coach, but the version that sits down next to you after a hard day.
**Music:** None.

---

## Script

[3-second silence]

The race is over. Whatever happened — however it went — the physical part is done. Now we process it. Not later. Not next week. Now, while it's fresh. Because the difference between rumination and reflection is structure. Rumination loops. Reflection closes.

This debrief has four questions. Take your time with each one. If you have a notebook or your phone, write your answers down. If you can't write right now, say them out loud. The act of articulating — out loud or in writing — is what makes this work. Thinking about it silently lets the chimp edit the story. Speaking or writing pins it down.

[3-second pause]

**Question One: What went well?**

Start here. Not with what went wrong — with what went right. Even if today was your worst race ever, something went well. Maybe you showed up. Maybe your nutrition plan held. Maybe you executed the first hour perfectly before things unraveled. Maybe you deployed the Grit Stack and it worked, even if the result didn't change. Maybe you kept going when everything told you to stop.

What went well today? Name at least three things.

[60-second pause]

If you're struggling to find three, you're letting the chimp filter your memory. The chimp only files the bad moments. Push past it. Something went well. Find it.

[20-second pause]

Good. Those answers? Add them to your Evidence File. They're proof. Even on a bad day, you accumulated evidence.

[5-second pause]

**Question Two: What was hard?**

Now we go there. What was genuinely difficult today? Not "everything" — that's not useful. What specific moments, decisions, or stretches were the hardest?

Was it physical — a section where your body hit a limit you didn't expect? Was it mental — a moment where the chimp nearly won, where you almost quit, where negative self-talk took over? Was it tactical — a decision you got wrong, a pacing error, a moment where you should have responded differently?

Name the hard parts. Be specific.

[60-second pause]

Notice how you feel as you name them. If there's frustration or disappointment — that's normal. Let it be there. Don't judge it. Just name the hard things honestly and move on to the next question.

[5-second pause]

**Question Three: What would I do differently?**

This is not the same as "what did I do wrong." This is forward-looking. Knowing what you know now — having lived this race — what would you change if you could do it again?

Maybe it's a pacing decision. Maybe it's a nutritional choice. Maybe it's a mental preparation gap — something you didn't practice enough, a tool you forgot to deploy, a scenario you didn't plan for. Maybe it's how you handled a specific moment.

Name one or two things. Not ten. Not a list of everything that went wrong. One or two actionable changes.

[45-second pause]

Whatever you just named — that's your next training focus. Not more miles. Not more intervals. The specific thing you'd do differently. That's where the growth lives.

[5-second pause]

**Question Four: What am I proud of?**

This is the one most athletes skip. Don't skip it.

Pride is not the same as satisfaction with the result. You can be proud of something even in a race that didn't go your way. You can be proud of showing up. Proud of how you handled adversity. Proud of executing a skill you've been practicing. Proud that you didn't quit when everything said to. Proud of how you treated yourself — or a competitor — in a difficult moment.

What are you proud of from today? Say it out loud. Or write it down.

[45-second pause]

Mean it. You earned it. Whatever today was — breakthrough or disaster or somewhere in the unspectacular middle — you did it. You showed up, you raced, and now you're sitting here doing a structured debrief instead of spiraling. That itself is a mental skill most athletes never develop.

[5-second pause]

**Closing the file.**

Four questions. What went well. What was hard. What you'd change. What you're proud of. That's the debrief.

Now close it. Literally. If you wrote it down, close the notebook or close the app. If you said it out loud, say one final sentence: "The race is processed. I'm done with it."

[5-second pause]

The race is processed. You're done with it.

Don't reopen it tonight. Don't replay it at dinner. Don't analyze your data until tomorrow at the earliest. The debrief captured what matters. Let the rest go.

You're not your last result. You're the athlete who shows up, performs, debriefs, and moves forward. That's who you are.

[3-second silence]

[End]
