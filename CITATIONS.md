## MODULE 1: THE TWO BRAINS

### Lesson 1.1: The Chimp and The Coach

**1.** Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux. | *Popularized dual-process theory: System 1 is fast, automatic, and emotional; System 2 is slow, deliberate, and logical — the foundational framework for understanding automatic vs. controlled thought in performance.*

**2.** Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases. *Science, 185*(4157), 1124–1131. | *The original empirical basis for dual-process thinking: people use mental shortcuts that are fast and efficient but produce predictable errors — the cognitive science behind "chimp" reactions.*

**3.** Frankl, V. E. (1946/1959). *Man's Search for Meaning*. Beacon Press. (English translation published 1959.) | *Frankl's core argument — that between any stimulus and response lies a human freedom to choose — is the philosophical foundation of the "stimulus-response gap" and the entire concept of the responding brain vs. the reacting brain.* Note: The specific aphorism "Between stimulus and response there is a space" is widely attributed to Frankl but its exact textual origin is disputed (Viktor Frankl Institute acknowledges this); the underlying concept is genuine Frankl doctrine from this work.

**4.** Peters, S. (2012). *The Chimp Paradox: The Mind Management Programme for Confidence, Success and Happiness*. Vermilion. | *Peters' applied model maps the emotional limbic system as "the Chimp" and the rational prefrontal cortex as "the Human," providing a practical framework that drove Team GB cycling gold medals at London 2012.*

### Lesson 1.2: Your Brain Is the Limiter

**1.** Noakes, T. D. (1997). 1996 J.B. Wolffe Memorial Lecture: Challenging beliefs — ex Africa semper aliquid novi. *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 29*(5), 571–590. | *The lecture that launched the Central Governor Theory: Noakes argued that fatigue is brain-regulated, not muscle-limited, and that an integrative neural governor prevents catastrophic physiological damage by reducing muscle recruitment before true exhaustion.*

**2.** Noakes, T. D., St Clair Gibson, A., & Lambert, E. V. (2005). From catastrophe to complexity: A novel model of integrative central neural regulation of effort and fatigue during exercise in humans — summary and conclusions. *British Journal of Sports Medicine, 39*, 120–124. | *The culminating paper in a five-article series (BJSM 2004–2005) formally describing the Central Governor Model: the brain maintains a "reserve" capacity and regulates exercise intensity via perceived effort, not peripheral muscle failure.*

**3.** Marcora, S. M., Staiano, W., & Manning, V. (2009). Mental fatigue impairs physical performance in humans. *Journal of Applied Physiology, 106*(3), 857–864. | *Seminal RCT showing that 90 minutes of prior cognitive work reduced time-to-exhaustion by ~18% with no change in cardiorespiratory physiology — proving perception of effort, not the muscles, is the performance limiter.*

**4.** Marcora, S. M., & Staiano, W. (2010). The limit to exercise tolerance in humans: Mind over muscle? *European Journal of Applied Physiology, 109*(4), 763–770. | *Demonstrated that maximal RPE (20) is reached at exhaustion while significant voluntary strength remains — directly evidencing the brain's protective "reserve" that the central governor model predicts.* `[needs verification — confirm exact journal volume/page]`

### Lesson 1.3: The Arousal Dial

**1.** Yerkes, R. M., & Dodson, J. D. (1908). The relation of strength of stimulus to rapidity of habit-formation. *Journal of Comparative Neurology and Psychology, 18*(5), 459–482. | *The original animal-learning experiment showing that moderate arousal optimizes performance while very high or very low arousal impairs it — the empirical origin of the inverted-U model, though Yerkes and Dodson did not use that term themselves.*

**2.** Hanin, Y. L. (1995). Individual zones of optimal functioning (IZOF) model: An idiographic approach to performance anxiety. In K. Henschen & W. Straub (Eds.), *Sport Psychology: An Analysis of Athlete Behavior* (3rd ed., pp. 103–119). Mouvement. | *IZOF established that optimal arousal is not universal but individual — each athlete has a unique anxiety bandwidth within which best performance occurs, measured retrospectively and prospectively.*

**3.** Hanin, Y. L. (2000). Emotions in sport. In Y. L. Hanin (Ed.), *Emotions in Sport* (pp. 65–89). Human Kinetics. | *Extended IZOF beyond anxiety to cover the full range of positive and negative emotions, establishing that individual emotional profiles predict performance quality — the scientific backbone of the "arousal dial" concept.* `[needs verification — confirm exact chapter details]`

**4.** Hanin, Y. L. (2015). The individual zones of optimal functioning (IZOF) model (1978–2014): Historical overview of its development and use. *International Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology, 15*(1), 41–53. | *Comprehensive retrospective confirming IZOF's evidence base across 35+ years of research and multiple sports — establishes individual arousal calibration as empirically sound practice.*

### Lesson 1.4: The 6-2-7 Breathing

**1.** Zaccaro, A., Piarulli, A., Laurino, M., Garbella, E., Menicucci, D., Neri, B., & Gemignani, A. (2018). How breath-control can change your life: A systematic review on psycho-physiological correlates of slow breathing. *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 12*, 353. | *Systematic review confirming that slow, paced breathing directly activates the parasympathetic nervous system by stimulating the vagus nerve, reducing heart rate, blood pressure, and cortisol — the physiological mechanism behind controlled breathing interventions.*

**2.** Laborde, S., Hosang, T., Mosley, E., & Dosseville, F. (2022). Effects of voluntary slow breathing on heart rate and heart rate variability: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, 138*, 104711. | *Meta-analysis confirming that voluntary slow breathing reliably increases vagally-mediated HRV, with effects present during a single session and accumulating with multi-session practice — validates breathing as a trainable arousal-regulation tool.*

**3.** Jerath, R., Edry, J. W., Barnes, V. A., & Jerath, V. (2006). Physiology of long pranayamic breathing: Neural respiratory elements may provide a mechanism that explains how slow deep breathing shifts the autonomic nervous system. *Medical Hypotheses, 67*(3), 566–571. | *Proposes the mechanistic model by which extended exhalation (longer than inhalation) specifically enhances parasympathetic tone — the biological rationale for asymmetric breathing patterns like 6-2-7.*

**4.** Swart, J., Lindsay, T. R., Lambert, M. I., Brown, J. C., & Noakes, T. D. (2012). Perceptual cues in the regulation of exercise performance — physical sensations of exercise and awareness of effort interact as separate cues. *British Journal of Sports Medicine, 46*(1), 42–48. | *Shows that breathing awareness and interoceptive attention are central to perceived effort regulation — making breath-focused practices directly relevant to pacing and performance endurance.* `[needs verification — confirm exact citation details]`

### Lesson 1.5: Attention — The Spotlight

**1.** Nideffer, R. M. (1976). Test of attentional and interpersonal style. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 34*(3), 394–404. | *The original paper establishing the two-dimensional model of attention (broad/narrow × internal/external) and the Test of Attentional and Interpersonal Style (TAIS) — foundation of all attentional focus research in sport.*

**2.** Morgan, W. P., & Pollock, M. L. (1977). Psychologic characterization of the elite distance runner. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences, 301*, 382–403. | *First systematic study of cognitive strategies in marathoners: elite runners used "association" (body-monitoring) while recreational runners dissociated — established the field of attentional focus research in endurance sport.*

**3.** Schücker, L., Hagemann, N., Strauss, B., & Völker, K. (2009). The effect of attentional focus on running economy. *Journal of Sports Sciences, 27*(12), 1241–1248. | *Demonstrated that an external attentional focus improved running economy (lower oxygen consumption) compared with internal focus on body movement — directly supporting external focus cuing for endurance athletes.*

**4.** Wulf, G. (2013). Attentional focus and motor learning: A review of 15 years. *International Review of Sport and Exercise Psychology, 6*(1), 77–104. | *Comprehensive 15-year review confirming that external focus consistently outperforms internal focus for motor performance and efficiency across skill levels, ages, and movement types — the definitive evidence base for attentional focus coaching.*

## MODULE 2: THE STORIES YOU TELL YOURSELF

### Lesson 2.1: The Identity Tree

**1.** Bandura, A. (1977). Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change. *Psychological Review, 84*(2), 191–215. | *The foundational paper introducing self-efficacy as a core psychological construct: people's belief in their capability to execute behaviours determines whether they initiate, persist, and succeed — the root of the "identity tree" model.*

**2.** Brewer, B. W., Van Raalte, J. L., & Linder, D. E. (1993). Athletic identity: Hercules' muscles or Achilles heel? *International Journal of Sport Psychology, 24*(3), 237–254. | *Introduced the AIMS scale and the concept that strong athletic identity predicts adherence and performance but also creates vulnerability — the dual-edged nature of sport-based self-definition.*

**3.** Markus, H. (1977). Self-schemata and processing information about the self. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 35*(2), 63–78. | *Established self-schemas as cognitive structures derived from past experience that filter and guide self-relevant information processing — the scientific basis of the "identity tree" metaphor: core beliefs shape what you notice, remember, and believe about yourself.*

**4.** Harter, S. (1990). Causes, correlates and the functional role of global self-worth: A life-span perspective. In R. J. Sternberg & J. Kolligian Jr. (Eds.), *Competence Considered* (pp. 67–97). Yale University Press. | *Distinguishes self-worth (global sense of value as a person) from self-esteem (domain-specific competence evaluations) — establishes why anchoring identity only to athletic performance creates fragility.* `[needs verification — confirm exact chapter details]`

### Lesson 2.2: Where Confidence Actually Comes From

**1.** Bandura, A. (1977). Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change. *Psychological Review, 84*(2), 191–215. | *The paper that identified the four sources of self-efficacy: mastery experiences (most powerful), vicarious experience, verbal persuasion, and physiological/emotional states — directly maps to the "four wells" of athletic confidence.*

**2.** Bandura, A. (1986). *Social Foundations of Thought and Action: A Social Cognitive Theory*. Prentice-Hall. | *Extended the self-efficacy framework to explain how performance accomplishments build the most durable confidence, and why failure at the wrong time can undermine it — the developmental theory behind confidence building.*

**3.** Moritz, S. E., Feltz, D. L., Fahrbach, K. R., & Mack, D. E. (2000). The relation of self-efficacy measures to sport performance: A meta-analytic review. *Research Quarterly for Exercise and Sport, 71*(3), 280–294. | *Meta-analysis of 45 studies confirming a significant positive relationship between self-efficacy and sport performance (r = .38 overall) — the quantitative evidence that confidence predicts results.*

**4.** Feltz, D. L., Short, S. E., & Sullivan, P. J. (2008). *Self-Efficacy in Sport*. Human Kinetics. | *Comprehensive sport-specific synthesis of Bandura's four sources, with empirical evidence for each in athletic populations — directly applicable to lesson content on building confidence through training progressions.* `[needs verification — confirm exact publication details]`

### Lesson 2.3: The Perfectionism Trap

**1.** Flett, G. L., & Hewitt, P. L. (2005). The perils of perfectionism in sports and exercise. *Current Directions in Psychological Science, 14*(1), 14–18. | *Identified the "perfectionism paradox": while athletes in many sports must deliver perfect outcomes, perfectionist personality traits tend to undermine performance through competitive anxiety and dissatisfaction — defines adaptive vs. maladaptive perfectionism in sport.*

**2.** Stoeber, J., & Childs, J. H. (2010). The assessment of self-oriented and socially prescribed perfectionism in sport: A perfectly fine distinction? *Psychology of Sport and Exercise, 11*(2), 188–194. | *Differentiated self-oriented perfectionism (setting high personal standards) from socially prescribed perfectionism (believing others demand perfection), showing the latter specifically predicts burnout, anxiety, and negative affect in athletes.*

**3.** Mosewich, A. D., Kowalski, K. C., Sabiston, C. M., Sedgwick, W. A., & Tracy, J. L. (2011). Self-compassion: A potential resource for young women athletes. *Journal of Sport & Exercise Psychology, 33*(1), 103–123. | *Demonstrated that self-compassion buffers the performance-impairing effects of perfectionism in female athletes — establishes self-compassion as the evidence-based alternative to the perfectionism trap.*

**4.** Gustafsson, H., Kenttä, G., & Hassmén, P. (2011). Athlete burnout: An integrated model and future research directions. *International Review of Sport and Exercise Psychology, 4*(1), 3–24. | *Systematic review linking maladaptive perfectionism (specifically evaluative concerns and concern over mistakes) to athlete burnout syndrome — the evidence that perfectionism is a burnout accelerant in endurance athletes.*

### Lesson 2.4: Performance Statements

**1.** Hayes, S. C., Strosahl, K. D., & Wilson, K. G. (1999). *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: An Experiential Approach to Behavior Change*. Guilford Press. | *The original ACT framework, introducing cognitive defusion (learning to observe thoughts as thoughts rather than facts) — the theoretical basis for "unhooking" from unhelpful performance narratives rather than fighting them.*

**2.** Hatzigeorgiadis, A., Zourbanos, N., Galanis, E., & Theodorakis, Y. (2011). Self-talk and sports performance: A meta-analysis. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 6*(4), 348–356. | *Meta-analysis of self-talk interventions confirming significant performance benefits (d = 0.48 overall); fine motor tasks benefited more (d = 0.67) than gross motor tasks (d = 0.26) — evidence that cue words and performance statements have real measurable effects.*

**3.** Theodorakis, Y., Weinberg, R., Natsis, P., Douma, I., & Kazakas, P. (2000). The effects of motivational versus instructional self-talk on improving motor performance. *The Sport Psychologist, 14*(3), 253–272. | *Experimental evidence that instructional self-talk improves fine motor/technical tasks while motivational self-talk benefits strength and endurance tasks — explains when to use technical performance statements vs. motivational cues.*

**4.** Tod, D., Hardy, J., & Oliver, E. (2011). Effects of self-talk: A systematic review. *Journal of Sport & Exercise Psychology, 33*(5), 666–687. | *Systematic review confirming self-talk's effectiveness as a sport intervention across multiple outcomes including performance, technique, anxiety regulation, and confidence — broad evidence base for performance statements.*

### Lesson 2.5: Athletic Schema Statement

**1.** Markus, H. (1977). Self-schemata and processing information about the self. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 35*(2), 63–78. | *Foundational self-schema theory: cognitive structures derived from past experience shape how people process new information, predict future behavior, and resist counter-schematic information — the science behind "who you believe you are shapes what you do."*

**2.** Brewer, B. W., Van Raalte, J. L., & Linder, D. E. (1993). Athletic identity: Hercules' muscles or Achilles heel? *International Journal of Sport Psychology, 24*(3), 237–254. | *Validated the AIMS (Athletic Identity Measurement Scale) and showed that athletic identity is measurable, stable, and predictive of sport behavior and coping — supports the rationale for consciously constructing an athletic schema statement.*

**3.** Merton, R. K. (1948). The self-fulfilling prophecy. *The Antioch Review, 8*(2), 193–210. | *The original formulation of self-fulfilling prophecy: a false belief, by influencing behavior, produces outcomes that confirm the original belief — the mechanism by which athletic schema statements (positive or negative) become self-reinforcing.*

**4.** Eccles, J. S., Wigfield, A., & Schiefele, U. (1998). Motivation to succeed. In W. Damon & N. Eisenberg (Eds.), *Handbook of Child Psychology* (5th ed., Vol. 3, pp. 1017–1095). Wiley. | *Expectancy-value theory evidence that self-concept of ability is the strongest predictor of persistence and achievement in challenging tasks — establishing why a well-formed identity statement is more than affirmation.* `[needs verification — confirm exact chapter details]`

Training the butterflies to fly in formation. *The Sport Psychologist, 13*(1), 22–41. | *Demonstrated that a structured coping imagery protocol — mentally rehearsing adverse scenarios and successful responses — reduced debilitative anxiety and improved performance in elite swimmers.* `[needs verification — confirm this is the specific coping imagery paper intended]`

### Lesson 3.4: Self-Talk — The Soundtrack

**1.** Blanchfield, A. W., Hardy, J., De Morree, H. M., Staiano, W., & Marcora, S. M. (2014). Talking yourself out of exhaustion: The effects of self-talk on endurance performance. *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 46*(5), 998–1007. | *The first RCT showing motivational self-talk improved cycling time-to-exhaustion by ~18% and significantly reduced RPE — directly links self-talk to the psychobiological model's central variable of perceived effort.*

**2.** Hatzigeorgiadis, A., Zourbanos, N., Galanis, E., & Theodorakis, Y. (2011). Self-talk and sports performance: A meta-analysis. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 6*(4), 348–356. | *Confirmed significant self-talk performance benefits across 32 studies; instructional self-talk works best for skill/technique tasks, motivational self-talk for endurance and strength — the match between self-talk type and task matters.*

**3.** Theodorakis, Y., Weinberg, R., Natsis, P., Douma, I., & Kazakas, P. (2000). The effects of motivational versus instructional self-talk on improving motor performance. *The Sport Psychologist, 14*(3), 253–272. | *Original experimental evidence distinguishing when motivational vs. instructional self-talk is more effective — foundational paper for matching the type of self-talk cue to the type of athletic demand.*

**4.** Kross, E., Bruehlman-Senecal, E., Park, J., Burson, A., Dougherty, A., Shablack, H., Bremner, R., Moser, J., & Ayduk, O. (2014). Self-talk as a regulatory mechanism: How you do it matters. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 106*(2), 304–324. | *Demonstrated that using one's own name and third-person pronouns in self-talk (distanced self-talk) produced better emotional regulation and performance than first-person self-talk — supports "talk to yourself like you'd talk to a teammate."*

### Lesson 3.5: Detachment in Action

**1.** Kabat-Zinn, J. (1990). *Full Catastrophe Living: Using the Wisdom of Your Body and Mind to Face Stress, Pain, and Illness*. Delacorte Press. | *Introduced and operationally defined mindfulness as "paying attention on purpose, in the present moment, and non-judgmentally" — the definitional foundation for all mindfulness-in-sport applications.*

**2.** Baer, R. A., Smith, G. T., Hopkins, J., Krietemeyer, J., & Toney, L. (2006). Using self-report assessment methods to explore facets of mindfulness. *Assessment, 13*(1), 27–45. | *The original Five Facets of Mindfulness Questionnaire paper (39 items, 5 factors: observe, describe, act with awareness, non-react, non-judge) — established the measurable, multi-dimensional structure of mindfulness that underpins sport psychology applications.*

**3.** De Petrillo, L. A., Kaufman, K. A., Glass, C. R., & Arnkoff, D. B. (2009). Mindfulness for long-distance runners: An open trial using Mindful Sport Performance Enhancement (MSPE). *Journal of Clinical Sport Psychology, 3*(4), 357–376. | *Open trial of the MSPE protocol with endurance runners: significant pre-to-post improvements in mindfulness, trait awareness, and sport-related worry — the first study testing a mindfulness protocol specifically with long-distance runners.*

**4.** Swann, C., Keegan, R., Piggott, D., & Crust, L. (2012). A systematic review of the experience, occurrence, and controllability of flow states in elite sport. *Psychology of Sport and Exercise, 13*(6), 807–819. | *Identified present-moment attentional focus as the most consistently reported precursor to flow in elite athletes — establishing mindfulness practice as the training ground for flow access.*

## MODULE 4: THE ZONE AND THE RED LINE

### Lesson 4.1: What Flow Actually Is

**1.** Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1975). *Beyond Boredom and Anxiety: Experiencing Flow in Work and Play*. Jossey-Bass. | *The original work introducing the flow concept — based on interviews revealing that total absorption in an activity produces a distinct, deeply rewarding psychological state characterized by effortlessness, loss of self-consciousness, and time distortion.*

**2.** Jackson, S. A., & Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1999). *Flow in Sports: The Keys to Optimal Experiences and Performances*. Human Kinetics. | *First book exclusively on flow in sport — identified and operationalized nine dimensions of flow, described challenge-skills balance as the primary trigger, and provided the framework for athletes to deliberately cultivate flow conditions.*

**3.** Jackson, S. A. (1996). Toward a conceptual understanding of the flow experience in elite athletes. *Research Quarterly for Exercise and Sport, 67*(1), 76–90. | *Qualitative study with elite athletes across seven sports: inductive content analysis confirmed that athletes' best performances mapped precisely onto Csikszentmihalyi's nine flow dimensions — established flow as scientifically measurable in sport.*

**4.** Jackson, S. A., & Marsh, H. W. (1996). Development and validation of a scale to measure optimal experience: The Flow State Scale. *Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology, 18*(1), 17–35. | *The validated psychometric instrument for measuring flow in sport (36 items, 9 subscales) — made flow researchable with rigorous scientific standards.*

---

### Lesson 4.2: Clutch vs Flow

**1.** Swann, C., Crust, L., Jackman, P., Vella, S. A., Allen, M. S., & Keegan, R. (2017). Performing under pressure: Exploring the psychological state underlying clutch performance in sport. *Journal of Sports Sciences, 35*(23), 2272–2280. | *Established clutch as a psychologically distinct state from flow: clutch involves heightened deliberate concentration, intense effort, and acute pressure awareness — whereas flow is effortless. Clutch is fatiguing; flow is energizing.*

**2.** Swann, C., Keegan, R., Crust, L., & Piggott, D. (2016). Psychological states underlying excellent performance in sport: Toward an integrated model of flow and clutch states. *Journal of Applied Sport Psychology, 28*(2), 1–21. | *Proposed an integrated model showing clutch and flow are two distinct pathways to excellent performance — clutch is driven by pressure and explicit effort, flow by challenge-skill balance and intrinsic engagement.*

**3.** Baumeister, R. F. (1984). Choking under pressure: Self-consciousness and paradoxical effects of incentives on skillful performance. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 46*(3), 610–620. | *The original "choking" paper: increased self-monitoring under pressure disrupts automated skilled performance — explains why the wrong attentional strategy in the deciding mile causes performance collapse rather than clutch performance.*

**4.** Beilock, S. L., & Carr, T. H. (2001). On the fragility of skilled performance: What governs choking under pressure? *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 130*(4), 701–725. | *Demonstrated that expert performers choke when pressure causes them to explicitly monitor automated skills — while novices choke when distracted. Established the expertise-reversal effect for attentional focus under pressure.*

### Lesson 4.3: Motivation

**1.** Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000). Self-determination theory and the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and well-being. *American Psychologist, 55*(1), 68–78. | *The landmark SDT summary paper: established that three innate psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) must be met to sustain intrinsic motivation — defines the conditions for durable, self-directed training motivation.*

**2.** Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (1985). *Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior*. Plenum. | *The foundational book articulating SDT — describes how the social environment either supports or undermines the three basic psychological needs, with direct implications for how coaches and training structures affect athlete motivation.*

**3.** Vallerand, R. J., Blanchard, C., Mageau, G. A., Koestner, R., Ratelle, C., Léonard, M., Gagné, M., & Marsolais, J. (2003). Les passions de l'âme: On obsessive and harmonious passion. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 85*(4), 756–767. | *The original Dualistic Model of Passion paper: harmonious passion (autonomous integration) predicts adaptive outcomes and sustainable performance; obsessive passion (controlled integration) predicts burnout, negative affect, and conflict — directly applicable to endurance athlete motivation management.*

**4.** Nicholls, J. G. (1984). Achievement motivation: Conceptions of ability, subjective experience, task choice, and performance. *Psychological Review, 91*(3), 328–346. | *Established task orientation (mastery, self-improvement) vs. ego orientation (outperforming others) as the core motivational distinction in achievement contexts — the scientific basis for shifting from outcome goals to process goals in endurance training.*

### Lesson 4.4: The Data Trap

**1.** Bischof, F., Tiemann, M., & Streicher, B. (2024).
